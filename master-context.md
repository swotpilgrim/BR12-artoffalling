# Master Context: Academy Romance Series

## Project Overview

This is a series of standalone clean YA romance novels set in timeless American boarding schools. Each novel is a complete one-shot with NO continuity between books—new school, new characters, fresh story every time. The series captures the classic boarding school romance atmosphere: preppy aesthetics, close-quarters living, academic intensity, and the unique intimacy of residential school life. Think Point Romance sophistication meets traditional prep school culture—timeless settings where romance develops through shared meals in dining halls, late-night study sessions, handwritten notes, and the contained world of campus life.

Target market: Readers seeking sophisticated YA boarding school romance with classic prep school atmosphere, academic stakes, and guaranteed happy endings.

## Genre & Editorial Standards

**Genre:** Young Adult Romance  
**Subgenre:** Clean/Sweet Boarding School Romance (timeless setting)  
**Content Level:** 
- Heat: Kissing only, emotional intensity, but age-appropriate
- Violence: None to minimal (school conflict, sports competition)
- Language: Sophisticated teen voice, no profanity
- Age-appropriateness: 14+ audience

**Editorial References:** Point Romance (Scholastic, 1990s-early 2000s) editorial conventions  
**Tone:** Sophisticated, emotionally mature, intellectually engaged, classic boarding school atmosphere

## Structural Requirements

**Novel Length:** 52,000 words  
**Chapter Count:** 30 chapters  
**Target Chapter Length:** 1,700 words  
**Structure:** Three-act romance structure

**Act Breakdown:**
- **Act 1 (Chapters 1-10): Setup & Connection** - 17,000 words
  - Introduction of protagonist and boarding school setting
  - Meet-cute or first encounter with love interest
  - Initial attraction/conflict established
  - First tentative connection made
  
- **Act 2 (Chapters 11-22): Development & Complications** - 20,400 words
  - Growing feelings and romantic tension
  - External obstacles (school rules, social hierarchies, academic pressure)
  - Internal obstacles (fear, insecurity, conflicting goals)
  - Midpoint turning point (emotional shift or revelation)
  - Dark moment or crisis point
  
- **Act 3 (Chapters 23-30): Resolution & Happy Ending** - 13,600 words
  - Confrontation of obstacles
  - Emotional truth/declaration
  - Resolution of conflict
  - Romantic payoff (kiss or significant romantic gesture)
  - Satisfying happy ending with clear commitment between couple

**Pacing Beats:** 
- Initial encounter by Chapter 3
- First meaningful interaction by Chapter 5
- Romantic tension escalating Chapters 6-15
- Midpoint emotional shift Chapter 15-16
- Crisis/dark moment Chapter 20-21
- Climactic resolution Chapter 28-29
- Happy ending Chapter 30

## Romance Pattern Elements

**Required Romance Tropes (rotate across series):**
- Academic competition/rivals-to-lovers
- Forced proximity (roommates, dorm neighbors, partners, teammates)
- Secret relationship (against school rules or social expectations)
- Class divide (scholarship vs. legacy students)
- New student/fish out of water
- Forbidden romance (headmaster's daughter, authority figure's child, social hierarchy)
- Opposites attract (arts vs. athletics, rule-follower vs. rule-breaker)
- Enemies-to-lovers
- Shared activity partnership (debate team, theater production, sports, school newspaper)
- Secret identity/anonymous connection

**Relationship Dynamics:**
- Age-appropriate teen protagonists (16-18 years old, typically juniors/seniors)
- School setting integration (romance develops through school life)
- Emotional connection prioritized over physical
- School rules and social hierarchies create obstacles
- Academic/extracurricular stakes give depth
- Mutual growth and understanding
- Clear romantic commitment by ending

**Romance Beats (Essential):**
1. Meet-cute or antagonistic first meeting
2. Initial attraction (may be reluctant or denied)
3. Obstacles appear (school rules, social differences, competition)
4. Moments of connection (building intimacy through proximity)
5. Romantic tension peaks
6. Crisis threatens relationship or forces choice
7. Emotional truth revealed
8. Romantic resolution
9. Happy ending confirmation

## Setting Requirements

**Time Period:** Timeless boarding school setting (Point Romance era aesthetic - 1990s/early 2000s feel)

**Location Type:** Co-ed boarding school (new school each book, various types)

**School Types to Rotate:**
- Academic prep schools (competitive, Ivy-focused)
- Arts academies (theater, music, visual arts boarding schools)
- Athletic prep schools (sports-focused)
- Traditional New England boarding schools (old money, legacy-heavy)
- Specialized schools (debate powerhouse, STEM-focused, etc.)

**Essential Setting Elements:**
- Dorm life (shared rooms, hallways, common rooms, limited privacy)
- Dining halls with assigned seating or house tables
- Campus libraries with study carrels and reading rooms
- School rules and curfews strictly enforced
- Academic/extracurricular pressure and competition
- Social hierarchies (legacy vs. scholarship, popular vs. outsider, different activities)
- Limited parental supervision but authority figures present
- School traditions and formal events
- Close-knit community feeling
- Pay phones in dorm hallways or limited phone access
- Physical mail and note-passing
- Dress codes (uniforms or preppy casual)

**Classic Boarding School Aesthetic (Required):**
- Preppy fashion: khakis, blazers, cardigans, oxford shirts, loafers
- Traditional architecture: brick buildings, ivy-covered walls, quad lawns
- Formal dining: sit-down meals, assigned tables, dress expectations
- Communication: handwritten notes, physical letters, face-to-face conversations
- School events: formals, debates, regattas, theatrical productions, athletics competitions
- Social spaces: common rooms, library study areas, campus grounds
- Technology: minimal—focus on direct interaction, not smartphones/social media
- Seasons matter: fall semester, winter formal, spring events, academic calendar rhythm
- House/dorm systems creating sub-communities
- Faculty living on or near campus
- Weekend activities and day student vs. boarder dynamics

**Point Romance Era Boarding School Atmosphere:**
- Timeless quality—not dated but not fully modern
- Sophisticated teen voices without current slang
- Romance develops through sustained proximity, not texting
- Notes slipped under doors or into lockers
- Meeting in physical spaces (library, common room, campus grounds)
- Longing and anticipation without instant communication
- School dances and formal events as major social occasions
- Academic achievement as genuine character motivation
- Extracurriculars as identity and social currency
- Authority figures as real obstacles (teachers, coaches, headmaster)
- Class distinctions visible (scholarship vs. legacy, old money vs. new)

## Character Archetypes

**Protagonist:** 
- Female or male, ages 16-18 (junior or senior year typically)
- Various backgrounds: scholarship student, legacy, new transfer, authority figure's child
- First-person POV preferred for Point Romance style (allows internal voice)
- Coming-of-age arc alongside romance arc
- Sophisticated but authentic teen voice
- Intellectually engaged with school life
- Growth toward confidence and self-knowledge

**Love Interest:**
- Ages 16-18
- Varies by romance pattern: academic rival, teammate, rule-breaker, legacy student, artistic opposite, mysterious transfer
- Appears incompatible or challenging initially
- Reveals depth and vulnerability
- Genuine emotional connection with protagonist
- Own character arc and motivations

**Supporting Cast (4-6 characters):**
- Roommate or best friend (confidante, advice-giver, voice of reason)
- Teacher, coach, or headmaster (authority figure, obstacle or ally)
- Rival or antagonist (romantic rival, academic competitor, social antagonist)
- Secondary friend group members (dorm mates, teammates, classmates)
- Each with distinct voice, function, and personality

## Continuity Model

**NO CONTINUITY BETWEEN BOOKS**

Each novel is completely standalone:
- New protagonist with new name
- New boarding school in new location with new name
- New supporting cast
- New romantic conflict and resolution
- Can reuse romance tropes but with different execution and characters

Readers should be able to pick up any book in any order without missing anything.

## Outliner Agent Deliverables

When assigned a book concept from the developed story seeds, produce THREE documents:

**1. BIBLE (2,500-3,000 words MAX - STRICT LIMIT)**
- Story premise (1 paragraph)
- Protagonist profile (150-200 words: background, personality, goal, fear/obstacle, arc)
- Love interest profile (150-200 words: background, personality, appeal, barrier, arc)
- 4-6 supporting character profiles (75-100 words each)
- Setting details specific to this novel (150-200 words: school name, type, location, key campus spaces, specific rules/traditions)
- Tone & voice guidelines for this story (100 words)
- Central theme (50-75 words)
- Romance trope(s) in play (50 words)

**2. MASTER OUTLINE (4,000-4,500 words: 30 chapters × 130-150 words each)**
- All 30 chapters summarized
- Each chapter: 130-150 words (NOT LESS, NOT MORE)
- Shows complete story arc from initial meeting to happy ending
- Tracks romantic relationship progression
- Indicates emotional beats and turning points
- Shows integration of school setting and academics/activities

**3. DETAILED CHAPTER OUTLINES (7,500-10,000 words: 30 chapters × 250-330 words each)**
- Beat-by-beat breakdown for each chapter
- Each chapter outline: 250-330 words
- Scene description, emotional beats, dialogue topics, chapter purpose
- Boarding school setting details (where scenes take place)
- Integration of academic/extracurricular elements
- Enough detail that scriptwriter expands through prose execution, not invention

**WORD COUNT DISCIPLINE IS MANDATORY - these limits ensure scriptwriter cognitive load remains manageable.**

## Success Criteria

A successful novel in this series will:
- Hit 52,000 words (±500 words acceptable)
- Deliver authentic boarding school atmosphere with Point Romance sophistication
- Maintain clean content parameters (kissing only, emotional intensity)
- Feature age-appropriate sophisticated teen voice
- Integrate school setting meaningfully (not just backdrop)
- Balance romance with academic/personal growth
- Create distinct, memorable characters
- Use school rules and social hierarchies as natural obstacles
- Provide emotionally satisfying romantic arc with clear happy ending
- Capture timeless boarding school aesthetic (preppy, traditional, classic)
- Maintain Point Romance era feel (sophisticated, no modern tech dependence)
- End with reader feeling satisfied and emotionally fulfilled
